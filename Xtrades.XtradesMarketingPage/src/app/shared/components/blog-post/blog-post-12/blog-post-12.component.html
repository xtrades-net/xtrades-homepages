Trading options is different than stocks, but can be incredibly rewarding $$$!

There are only 2 kinds of options: calls and puts. Learn stock market trading with this full guide on trading options for beginners, where we’ll cover how they work and how you can get started leveraging these amazing tools of modern Finance for your personal benefit.

A call gives you the right to call shares at an agreed strike price. This means that regardless of the current market price for a particular stock, you have the right to call those shares at whatever price your call specifies. So for example if you have a call with a strike price of $50 per share and the current market price is $100 per share, then you have the right to call or buy those shares at a $50 each. This of course means you can turn around and sell those shares in the market for $100 each and profit $50 per share. Now because options are traded in lots of 100 shares each, this means that you would profit $50 * 100 shares equals $5,000!

What this means is that if you are the owner of a call with a strike price which is lower than the current market price then you stand to make a profit. In other words if you buy the $50 strike price today and the market climbs to $100 per share then you make money.

This is what it looks like in a graphical format:

Here we have the stock price on the horizontal axis with X being the strike price of your car, so I assume that X equals $50. On the vertical axis you have profit or loss, where profit is anything above and loss is anything below the zero or horizontal axis. If the stock price moves up from 50 to 100 then your profit will be equal to whatever the green arrow shows. Whereas if the stock goes down below $50 then you lose. First thing that should stand out is that the profit is infinite because the stock price can move up infinitely, in theory. However the loss is limited to whatever the value you paid to own that call.

The end your break even point is going to be the strike price of the call plus whatever you paid for it. This makes sense because if you paid $2 for the call than the price of the underlying has to be equal to the strike price, $50 plus the two dollars you paid for the call.

Example:
Buy a $50 Strike Call for $2 in TSLA
Breakeven for TSLA must be $50 + $2 = $52 for you to profit

The put is basically the opposite transaction. A put allows you to essentially “put“ to someone at the agreed strike price, regardless of the market price for the underlying. This means that if you have a put with a strike price of $100 per share and the current market price has dropped to $50 per share, then you can put to someone at $100 even though current market price is $50!

So in this case you profit when the price of the underlying drops below X which is the strike price. Therefore the green arrow represents your profit as the value of the underlying drops. On the other hand if the price of the underlying goes in the other direction, in other words up, then you lose only as much as you paid for that put. And of course the breakeven will be the strike price minus the value you paid for the put.

Example:
Buy a $50 Strike Put for $2 in AAPL
Breakeven for AAPL must be $50 - $2 = $48 for you to profit

THEORY

Although it can be boring, theory is important. You have to understand how options get their value if you’re going to try and predict whether the price of those options is going to go up or down. Once you can figure that out, the bet is basically a no-brainer. This is more complicated than beginner day trading with stocks, because Options are more volatile and require a deeper understanding.

It turns out that options have value for two reasons: intrinsic value and extrinsic value. As the names suggest, intrinsic value is the value given to the option by virtue of what is inside the option contract itself. What is inside the options contract is the ticker of the underlying, the strike price and as we will learn later the expiry date. In this case it is the strike price which starts off by giving the options contract value. This is because if the strike price of the call is $50 and the current market price is $52 then you could stand to profit by exercising the car, receiving those 100 shares by paying $50 each and then turning around and selling them at market price of $52 each. This would net you two dollars per share or $200 in total. Therefore the intrinsic value of that option contract is two dollars.

Extrinsic value is given by factors that are external to the options contract itself such as whatever can happen in the future to the value of the underlined. This is where the famous Greeks come into play. The two most important Greeks are Theta and Vega. Theta represents time and Vega represents volatility.

It turns out that an option that expires in two or three days has very little external factors that can affect the price of the underlying and make that price of the underlying rocket up or dump down. On the other hand an option that expires in 2 to 3 months has a lot more vulnerability to different external factors such as inflation, lawsuits, earnings, buyouts etc. That is why time affects the value of an option contract. Volatility comes into play because the market itself is prone to ups and downs for different reasons and when traders begin to buy up or sell off a particular underlying it creates volatility in the sense that there is a greater uncertainty as to where the underlined price will end up at. And those are the two main reasons why the price of options fluctuate. It is important for you to understand these two Greeks, as well as the others we will see later, in order for you to correctly determine whether the options contract is going to go up or down.

As you can see a $195 strike Call has an intrinsic value of about $6 if the price of that underlying is $201. This is the case for when we have 45 days to expiration of the contract. As you can see on the chart above the stock price is approximately $201 and therefore the intrinsic value which is the green line on the bottom chart is about $6.

As time goes by the price of the underlying fluctuates and it moves exactly two $195 at which point the bottom chart with the price of the option as given by the intrinsic value drops to zero because there is no longer any price difference between the strike and the current market price. However the value of the option has not dropped to zero completely because it still has extrinsic value which is given by the pinkish area and at that point the price of the call is approximately four dollars. This is because with so much time left until expiration there is still a chance for the price of this stock to go up above $195 per share. As you can see approximately 20 days to expiration the price goes back and touches $195 per share however the price of the call is only $2. That is still due to the extrinsic value given by the fact that there are approximately 20 days for the stock to climb above $195 per share. But as time dwindled down to nine days or less you can see that even as the stock price tries to recover back around $193 the value of that call barely moves above zero which is indicative of the fact that it is unlikely the price will climb above $195. At that point the price of the underlying would have to shoot up above $195 in order to have any value at all.

Time Decay

The important thing to remember about time decay is that it eats away at the value of your option which is called premium. During the first 120 to 90 days the amount of premium that decays is very little but as time goes by the amount of option value that is eaten away by the passage of 30 days becomes greater. This means that the closer to expiration, the quicker an option will lose its total value and must rely on intrinsic value alone in in order to be worth something. This means that contracts about to expire must experience very strong moves in the underlying in order to profit.

Terminology

Let’s put together some of the terminology that will help us further discuss training options and how to actually make money from them.

If you predict that a stock is going to go up in the next three months then you decide to buy a call option which will benefit if the price of the underlying goes above the strike price. The closer you get to expiration the higher above the strike price you have to be in order to profit because there is no more time and by definition no more volatility left, and intrinsic value is the only friend you have.

Extra Tips

Another extra tip for you guys is do not get enticed by smaller sized contracts that are further out of the money. It is very common to want to buy five or 10 cheaper contracts just because they are further out of the money. Remember that out of the money means that the probability of the underlying reaching that strike price is much lower. As a matter of fact the Delta is often referred to as the probability of that contract ending in the money. An in the money contract has a delta possibly greater than 50 whereas out of the money contracts have Delta’s less than 30. Yes out of the money contracts are cheaper but it is more likely that you will end up with a worthless option on expiry.

Another important tip is to think about your profits an app percentage return instead of absolute value return. Let me give you an example. If you invest $100 and you make $15 then you should exit because this is a 15% return, which is way better than the average market return. If you were expecting to make $100 in profit instead of $15, do not expect your $100 investment to double into $200, which would mean a 100% return. The reason is that a 100% return is much less likely to occur and therefore you are very likely to end up losing your original investment then actually doubling it. If for some reason you are expecting $100 in return then you would have to invest $1000 and expect the 10% return instead.

You must first pick a stock to trade. You can either get this from a trade signal at X trades or you could search for your own Stocks using Stocks scanning tools provided by your broker. You could also just look for a stock and begin charting it but you might end up charging a bunch of stocks that don’t give you a definitive direction as to where they might be going.

It would then be useful to know what the current market conditions are, economic factors that are currently pushing up or pushing down the economy and the particular market sector in which your ticker is operating. This is important because if you are trading a ticker that is being negatively affected by the overall industry, say for example the healthcare industry is in trouble because of regulations from the government for example then it will be much harder for a healthcare ticker to move upwards while the entire sector is dragging it down. In that case you might actually try for buying puts instead.

Once you have decided on a specific ticker then of course you must chart it using support and resistance lines as well as trend lines and indicators that will give you an idea of where that stock might be headed.

Finally you take your position using the risk reward position tool in order to gauge your target price and stop-loss price and you follow it to a tee.

Finally whether you won or lost, but especially if you lost, you must review the trade and try to understand what happened. It is just as important to understand why you lost as it is to simply win. You may have one out of luck and that is not very helpful. If anything that is actually counterproductive because you might think you know what you’re doing and put on a bigger trade the next time and lose big. So always make sure to go back and understand what went wrong; whether it was something out of your control or not, you will learn something from a losing trade.
