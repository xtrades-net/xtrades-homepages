The market moves fast, you need the tools to keep up with it.

The markets move quickly, whether you're trading stocks, options, futures or even cryptocurrency. Especially in the case of options, a small, sudden movement on a stock price can be the difference between a losing trade and one that's profitable.

But given that traders, like everybody else, have other work and people they want to spend time with, how is it possible to stay on top of price movements without being stuck staring at your computer monitor or smartphone screen all day?

Traders who want to have a life outside of trading must master the use of real-time alerts. By setting up parameters for when you get those alerts, what information they contain, and what actions should be taken on those alerts, you can free up time and attention without missing a key trading moment.

At Xtrades, analysts and staff traders set up alerts that give them an edge over the market; a couple of minutes or even precious seconds can help maximize profits on a trade or avoid big losses when there's a drastic price change.

There are lots of ways to set up alerts and use them. We'll go over a few easy ways to start utilizing real-time alerts, whether they're real-time stocks trackers, real-time stock news alerts, or real-time trade alerts, for your trading activity.

Setting up basic pricing alerts

We'll start by talking about the most basic type of alert, which is very commonly used by traders and may be the most useful for day-to-day monitoring of the market and assets: price alerts.

Let's say you are an investor in Tesla (TSLA) and you know that you'd like to sell some of your stock at a certain price, say $250 a share. You could set an order with your broker to sell your stock at that price. But setting an alert using your broker's software, an app, or charting software like TradingView, might be better. When you get the alert on your smartphone, as a pop-up on your computer, or as an email, you can decide what action to take. You might notice that Tesla has made a huge announcement or that the stock is trading at very high volumes, which might prompt you to hold on to the stock a little longer to see what happens.

By the same token, a sudden downswing on a stock or the market as a whole might be useful if you've set an alert on an index such as SPY, which is made up of tech-sector stocks. An alert about a massive drop in SPY might clue you in on changes in the tech sector that might warrant selling some of your holdings before things get worse. That same alert might also be a signal to consider buying in on a dip if you think a rebound is coming.

Some advanced traders also set up alerts based on more sophisticated conditions than a single stock ticker's movement. "Scanners" are tools that can spot trends and sometimes help pick out good stocks and options to invest into.

Alexander_96, a senior analyst at Xtrades, suggests setting up alerts for prices near gaps on stock's chart: those are areas where the stock had a sudden upward or downward jump that left a gap in pricing.

"Setting price alerts at unfilled gaps is a good way to have potential trade setups for the future," he says. "Gap-fill trades can have consistent and fast momentum once price has entered the unfilled price zone."

Keeping up with news alerts

Photo by nappy

If you follow a specific set of stocks or a business sector, news alerts are another key tool. While some traders focus on technical analysis of a stock's price movement, knowing the fundamentals of a company, including its financial health and news such as product announcements and upcoming earnings report events, can also give you an edge.

Financial websites such as Seeking Alpha and Benzinga focus on business news, but you can also set up news alerts on the Wall Street Journal, Barron's and even Google News and Apple News. For these sites you might set up keyword alerts or alerts based on the stock tickers you want to follow.

Not all news creates big pricing moves in stocks, but typically earnings reports and news of things like a stock split or an acquisition can create big swings in the value of a stock.

Trade alerts keep you on top of your accounts

The last type of alerts we'll discuss are those involving your own holdings; these are trade alerts you'd set up on your broker. The options for alerts vary from broker to broker, but most offer a way to stay up to speed on what's happening with your holdings, whether it's through emails, daily confirm statements or push alerts to your phone.

Some types of alerts you might want to set up with your broker include:

  • Any time stocks or options are sold from your account.
  • Orders that have been canceled or expired.
  • Large percentage changes in stocks that you trade. Some brokers such as Firstrade send these alerts by default to users of their mobile app.
  • Trading halts on stocks that you own or are interested in following.

Setting trade alerts, whether they're based on pricing, news, or your own brokerage account activity, are a good way to stay informed as a trader without devoting every moment of the day to watching the markets.
