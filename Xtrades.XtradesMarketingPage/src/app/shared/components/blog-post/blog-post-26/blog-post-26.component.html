A Brief Summary Of Basic Risks

Trading options for beginners can be exciting and potentially lucrative but comes with some risks. You need to understand some crucial risks and ways to minimize them so you don’t end up underwater with too many risks.

Risk Of Option Contracts

The market for options can be highly volatile, increasing your level of risk exposure. For example, our YouTube video on How To Manage Risk With Options Trading illustrates how a 1% move in a stock could equal up to a 50% move in an options contract.

Risk management is integral to your success. Whether you’re an active trader or looking at trading options for beginners, it should not be overlooked.

This is because traders risk incurring losses in the market, which can lead to severe financial losses. So, regardless of your experience level and ability at trading, a proper risk management strategy must be employed to manage and protect the money in the trader's account.

This includes having a risk-reward ratio for each trade to protect against possible losses. It also involves ensuring that one never puts all their capital into one trade, as this can be disastrous if the outcome does not go according to expectations.

Beware The Theta

Theta is best defined as the estimated decline rate of any option's value over time. If all the variables remain constant, an option will lose value over time as the contract matures.

Typically, Theta is expressed as a negative number that calculates an option's loss rate for more extended positions and indicates the devaluing an option experiences daily until it matures.

Because the Theta can impact an option contract, an important concept to consider is the correct expiration date, which is crucial. If you’re doing day trading, a weekly contract is OK, whereas a monthly contract at a maximum of 2-3 months is also acceptable with swing trading.

For example, if you’re looking at a different day and swing trading ideas and choose to swing trade, don’t use a weekly contract because even if the movement is fast, the Theta is still too much of a burden and will kill your investment. In contrast, a day trading strategy that includes a weekly contract is acceptable.

A call is when the contract is due, and you will either make a profit or pay your losses.

The three results when a call happens include:

  1. Up: Opportunity to make much money unless Theta impacts the contract terms.
  2. Flat: Money stays as is; there is not much risk of loss, but there is not much to gain.
  3. Down: Potential for significant losses, mainly if not managed properly.

The 1% Rule

A trading strategy to minimize risk is known as the one-percent rule. This strategy helps manage risk by stating that you should never invest or trade anything higher than 1% of your total capital on any single position.

This strategy lets you quickly diversify your investment portfolios, limiting risk exposure and creating better-earning potential. As a general rule, the one-percent rule is one that both day and swing traders with smaller account balances can safely trade without getting too overexposed and risking the shock of a large call against your account.

A Stop-Loss Strategy

In every trader’s options strategy, you want to have a stop-loss in place. Since trading can be volatile, having protection against severe swings will prevent losing your shirt if the market drops. It’s a tool to protect against losses, and it works by setting a price to trigger your sale.

Set A Price: Once you have your investment going, set a price point so that when a stock drops below, it triggers an automatic sale.

This stop-loss strategy can also prevent traders from holding onto investments too long with the delusion that they may rebound in value, which protects against significant losses.

  • Use Paper Trades To Test
  • Diversify Your Portfolio
  • Start Small
  • Set Stop-Loss Orders
  • Maintain Discipline
  • Stay Informed
