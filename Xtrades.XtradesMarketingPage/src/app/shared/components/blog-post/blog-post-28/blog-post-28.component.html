Getting started with trading scripts

First off: don't be afraid to ask, "What exactly are trading scripts?" Scripts are basically any software tool, like say an algorithm, that can give you useful data or visuals you need to help you make decisions on stock, options or futures trading. That's it. You might also hear the term "Indicators" used to describe scripts. Typically an indicator is a tool that you'd use on a stock chart that would give you specific information overlaid on that chart.

For instance, you might find a script that world on the popular charting software TradingView that uses "Bollinger Bands" methodology. A Bollinger Bands script would combine a moving average line on the chart for a stock that you're following and would also contain two standard-deviation lines that would help you see when a stock is being oversold or overbought.

Using settings for that script (also known as an "indicator" like we mentioned), you might be able to adjust the length of time for the middle line's moving average and how much standard deviation you want to see on the chart. By studying a chart with this information, you might have a better idea of when to enter a trade (if, say, a stock price is rebounding from the lower trend line) or when to sell (when the stock price is not able to clear the upper trend line).

How to explore channels for stock-market trading

When you hear "Channels," you might think of television or different parts of a Discord server.

But in stock trading, a channel is a pricing range that a stock, option or other asset is trading in. That accounts for its rises and falls and can be really useful in determining whether a stock is poised to break out of its pattern positively, or if it might fall below that range.

While a stock might be in a longer-term rising or falling pattern, it could still go up and down in the short term. Channels help us see what the overall trend is and whether it's worth trading long or short on that stock or option.

In this chart, we can see that the asset's channel is in a downward trend.

A few reasons to watch channels for stocks include:

  • Easily visualizing a stock's upper channel, where it might be overbought and ready to come back down.
  • Easily visualizing a stock's lower channel, where it might be oversold and ready to bounce back up.
  • Spotting trends where a stock could be breaking out of its channel. These can be good opportunities to make money on a long position when a stock breaks out above the trend line or to profit from shorting when it falls below the trend.
