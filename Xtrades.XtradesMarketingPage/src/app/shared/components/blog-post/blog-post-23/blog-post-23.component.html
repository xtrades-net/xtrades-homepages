What you need first

It might seem obvious, but the first things you need to start stock trading are money, time, and a broker to execute your trades.

The great news is you don't need a lot of money. Some brokerage firms offer promo deals, including free stocks, just for signing up. You can get your feet wet for as little as $20 to $50 by investing in stocks that don't cost much. There are lots of ways to use that money, from basic trading stocks, options and futures, to complex strategies that involve selling derivatives or betting against a company's fortunes on the stock market, but to start, you'll want to have an understanding of basic stock trading (also known as "equities").

Buying stock in a company just means owning a share of that company, whether it's Tesla or Apple or a much-smaller startup, but there are different types of stocks and they vary in price, voting rights and dividends, the payouts that companies make to shareholders when they're successful.

You don't need a big investment to start buying and trading these shares, or fractions of these shares, but you will need time. You'll want to learn some of the basics of how stocks work and to gain some fundamental skills that will help keep you from losing the money you're investing. The most successful traders we've encountered take the time to learn about the companies they're trading and carefully study the movement of stocks with charting software (we'll get to that) and indicator tools that help alert them to shifts and trends in the stock market.

Lastly, you'll need to choose a broker, or multiple brokers, that can execute your trades. Some of the most well-known brokers include TD Ameritrade, Robinhood, Fidelity, and Charles Schwab. A broker you sign up with will typically ask for verification of your identity, your level of experience as a trader and whether you want a cash, margin or long-term investment account such as an IRA.

Types of broker accounts

Some brokerages allow you to open up multiple accounts or to switch from cash to margin if you change your mind later.

  • A cash account allows you to trade with whatever funds you have in your account, but there are some drawbacks. When you buy and then sell a stock, you have to wait for funds from the sale to settle before you can use that money to buy more stocks. It usually takes two trading days for funds to settle.
  • A margin account gives you a little more flexibility; you're basically borrowing funds on credit, so you have more buying power and you don't have to wait for funds to settle before you can trade again. But margin's not free: you have to pay interest fees and if you incur enough losses, the brokerage can sell stocks in your account or ask you to deposit more money to meet a certain threshold. Another disadvantage for margin accounts: day trading is much more limited unless you have more than $25,000 in your account. If you don't, you're limited to three day trades (buying and selling a stock or option in the same day) within five business days.
    If you go over those three trades, your account can be flagged and you can be put in a trading time-out for 90 days. Cash accounts don't have that day-trading limit.
  • IRA or custodial accounts (if you wanted to start accounts for your kids, for instance) are used for long-term investments; there may be penalties for withdrawing funds early. But at a lot of brokerages, they work just like cash or margin accounts; you can execute the same types of trades and even trade cryptocurrency.

Getting educated about stocks

Xtrades offers educational channels on the Discord, a YouTube channel with lots of videos on trading strategies, and blog posts like this one. The day-to-day discussion that goes on in these venues, plus the alerts put out by analysts, staffers, and members are good ways to immerse yourself in the culture and best practices of stock and options traders.

Unlike some things in life, with stock trading it's not always best to just dive in and learn by doing. That's a good way to lose all the money in your account before you even know what you're doing. Because you have actual money at stake, you'll want to at least understand the basics of stock trading before you start risking funds you'd like to keep.

Your broker will have built-in charting tools and software. Study the price movements of stocks you're interested in. Is the stock trending upward long-term? Does it tend to spike in the mornings when the market opens? More advanced traders use dedicated chart software such as TradingView that can incorporate lots of advanced tools for tracking the stock market. You can get a good Black Friday deal on TradingView's services every year.

One good way to learn how to trade without financial risk is called "Paper trading." Lots of brokers allow you to create a paper trading account that allows you to trade virtually: you can buy and sell to your heart's content, but you're not using real money; the trades and the funds are virtual. Paper trading is a good way to see if strategies your learning would actually play out and to start learning more advanced trading such as options without the huge risk involved of committing money before you really know what you're doing. We do not recommend trading options before you have a fundamental understanding of common stock trading.

Advice from the top

Xtrades founder Kevin Wan says that new traders can get a head start on success by keeping a few things in mind:

  • Keep emotions in check. "I think a lot of beginner traders make consecutive mistakes due to emotional reaction to losses or inability to accept a loss and move onto the next trade," Wan says.
  • See the big picture. New, inexperienced traders may only be focused on one or two stocks and unable to play the law of numbers by having a larger sample of trades play out in their favor. "Most beginner traders fall victim to focusing too hard on their current results instead of focusing on finding a consistent, applicable, and repeatable strategy that works for them, and can outlast the market's irrationality," Wan says.
  • Be more selective. Wan says, "I think traders would benefit greatly if, out of any 10 to 20 trade opportunities they see, be able to pick and choose just 1 or 2 out of that selection and only take those trades. Practice trade selection and focus on getting better and sharper at developing strategies around this when you're starting."
